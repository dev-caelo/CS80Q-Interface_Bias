Hello there! This webpage showcases some common forms of interface biases found throughout the web.
This page serves as a PSA aiming to promote further diversity and inclusion.

This webpage was created as my final project for Cynthia Lee's CS80Q: Race and Gender in Silicon Valley.

Radios and Non-Binary Genders

Reference: Sabrina Fonseca

Choose your Gender: *

Binary Gender Choice

This form is unfortunately the most common implementation of this question throughout the web. Although it may be difficult to represent every possible gender affiliation on a registration form, supporting only a binary radio selection between Male and Female is an injustice given how much more flexible and representative these next implementations can be. We must do better than this!

What is your Gender:

I'd Prefer Not

Unless this information is absolutely essential, users should be given the choice whether or not to disclose their gender identity. While this implementation provides better representation for genderqueer/non-conforming, the simplicity of the radio-style form limits this representation to a single, general option for a wide range of gender identities. Still much more preferable to the binary form!

What are your Pronouns?

Asking for Pronouns Instead

Unless gender information is essential, it may be more appropriate to ask for a user's pronouns as opposed to their gender. Taking from platforms such as Instagram and LinkedIn, pronouns can be listed by users through a textbox input. As opposed to radio-style buttons, this implementation gives users the freedom to correctly identify their preferred pronouns, while also avoiding the problem of not representing a user's pronouns in a radio-style list.

What is your Gender?

Maybe Don't Ask?

As Sabrina explains, there are many times where this information is not even necessary to ask in the first place. Simplifying the process of filling out a form by avoiding the question entirely, this implementation avoids forcing users to answer this complicated question. Quite simply, unless this information is truly necessary to track (and if so, the reasoning should be explicitly stated to the user), there is no reason to ask in the first place.

One Letter Name Biases

Reference: Cynthia Lee and E

Short Name Bias

Many account creation schemes tend to deny names less than three characters long. For many sites, these shorter names may pose a problem to searching and indexing for each of these users in their database. But this is not a reason to disenfranchise a user's representation!
Systems should be fitted to allow these short names through, no strings attached!

Password Substring

Ironically, for sites that do allow one or two character length names, password creation becomes unintentionally challenging. Some sites tend to check whether or not a user's first or last names are part of their password, denying passwords that include this information for account security. However, this poses a huge problem for short names that may find themselves losing out a whole letter to create their password with!

Forms and Race

What is your Race/Ethnic Group: *

Multi-Racial and Ethnic Representation

Although one's racial/ethnic identity may be difficult to fully represent on such a simple interface, forms should strive to be as inclusive and representative as possible. The sample above is modified from Google Careers' application form, providing flexibility with checkboxes over radios and the option to not disclose entirely.

Where from Asia:

Asian Mis-Representation

As a Filipino myself, there are a number of times where I simply cannot choose my own ethnicity after specifying that I am Asian. This sample clearly shows that, while also forgetting Laos, Myanmar/Burma, and Mongolia to name a few. In addition, lists like these are confusing as they are intended to encapsulate one's ethnicity, but there are situations where ethnicity may not be obvious, such as with Taiwan and Singapore.

Where from Asia:

Regions over Ethnicity

If possible, users should have the option of choosing the region in which they have descended from. Sorry for the odd wording, but first chunking out Asia into smaller subregions and then specifying ethnicities within those regions would make the form much easier to use. In addition, by subdividing Asia into each region, it allows for the form to appear less cluttered while also improving on representation!

Name Change Limitations

Source: Penelope Phippen

Why is it so Difficult?

As Penelope writes, many system implementations make it extremely difficult or outright impossible to change one's name with. Outside of creating a whole new account, users tend to have little to no recourse for implementing a name change. This especially affects the transgender community as they transition away from their dead-names.
